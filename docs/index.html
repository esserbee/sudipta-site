About Me

Bridging academic research and industry innovation in optical systems and semiconductor devices.

I'm a Hardware Engineer at Meta, where I work on image sensor validation for AR/VR devices. My work spans the full lifecycle from design validation to production testing, ensuring world-class image quality and sensor performance.

Previously, I was an Optical Sensing Hardware Engineer at Apple focused on ambient light sensor test and validation, and contributed to LED innovation at Lumileds, bringing deep expertise in photonics and semiconductor physics to cutting-edge product development. At Lumileds, I co-invented patents spanning microlens arrays, air-spaced optics, pcLED structures, and metalens-based optical designs.

I hold a PhD in Electrical Engineering from the University of Minnesota, where I worked under Prof. Tony Low on theoretical and computational studies of two-dimensional materials and nanophotonic devices. My research has been published in top journals including Nature Communications and Physical Review, with over 450 citations.

I received my BSc from Bangladesh University of Engineering and Technology (BUET) and was awarded the prestigious 3M Science and Technology Doctoral Fellowship during my graduate studies.

When I'm not working on sensors or simulations, you'll find me playing tennis, hiking trails, or experimenting with ukulele and violin.

Graphene Metasurfaces

Electrically tunable graphene-based metasurfaces for beam steering, cloaking, and wave manipulation.

Tunable Graphene Metasurface Reflectarray

This work showed how electrically tunable graphene metasurfaces can dynamically steer beams, focus energy, and even produce cloaking-style optical effects. I combined electromagnetic simulation with programmable phase control to establish a design framework for active metasurfaces in future photonic and RF systems.

143 citations · Physical Review Applied

View Publication

Graphene Plasmonic Metasurface for Beam Forming

I developed theoretical models showing how graphene plasmons can support reconfigurable beam forming while also enabling sensing functionality on the same platform. The study highlighted how gate-tunable plasmon resonances can turn a single metasurface into a multifunctional device.

View Publication

Tunable Nonlinear Optics in 2D Materials

Band nesting effects enabling enhanced and tunable nonlinear optical responses in layered materials.

Double Resonant Second Harmonic Generation via Band Nesting

This work showed that band nesting in layered materials can strongly enhance second harmonic generation when the optical excitation is resonant. The result connected electronic band structure directly to nonlinear optical response and suggested a route to tunable frequency-conversion devices.

Physical Review B

View Publication

Tunable Hyperbolicity and Nonlinear Response in 2D Materials

I explored how electronic-structure engineering can induce tunable hyperbolic dispersion and strong anisotropic nonlinear response in two-dimensional materials. These findings helped bridge material band structure, optical dispersion, and metasurface-enabled control in a single framework.

View Publication

Plasmon-based Gas Sensing

Graphene plasmons for highly sensitive and selective molecular detection through infrared fingerprinting.

Gas Identification with Graphene Plasmons

This project demonstrated label-free infrared gas detection by matching molecular fingerprints to graphene plasmon resonances. By linking adsorption to the sensing signal, the work showed a path to highly sensitive detection without chemical functionalization.

257 citations · Nature Communications

View Publication

Plasmonic Gas Sensing with Graphene Nanoribbons

I investigated localized graphene nanoribbon plasmons for molecular sensing, where strong near-field confinement boosts interaction with target gases. The analysis showed how nanoscale geometry can improve sensitivity and make plasmonic sensors more compact and selective.

Undergraduate Research: III-V Transistor Modeling

Quantum ballistic simulations of next-generation III-V semiconductor devices at BUET.

III-V Quantum Well MOSFET Modeling

This undergraduate project used quantum ballistic simulations to study InGaAs/InAs quantum well MOSFETs. I analyzed transport and capacitance-voltage behavior to understand how III-V channels could scale toward next-generation transistor technologies.
